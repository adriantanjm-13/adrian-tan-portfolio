1Context2Discovery3Defining4Blueprint5Design6Road AheadContextDeprecating "Admin"Upon joining Oxwash in September 2022, my primary project was the development of a new web-based platform called "Mercury." The motivation behind creating this platform stemmed from the realization that the existing "Admin" platform was no longer effective in terms of its functionality. To provide context, the Admin platform had been externally built by a design agency back in 2019.
Over time, the platform team received substantial criticism regarding the limitations of the current platform. Additionally, it became evident that the existing tool heavily relied on third-party tools to support our operational activities. As a result, it became imperative for us to address these challenges while building Mercury, and we established two primary goals: (1) addressing the criticisms of the "Admin" platform, and (2) reducing dependency on third-party tools.

DiscoveryUnderstanding the problemIn order to undertake the development of Mercury, it was crucial for us to fully comprehend the underlying reasons behind the need for this project. Obtaining buy-in from stakeholders was essential before committing extensive time and resources to such a significant undertaking. As a result, between September and December 2022, I took the lead in conducting a thorough discovery process alongside a junior product designer and our project manager. Our aim was to identify the pain points and challenges associated with Mercury.
Throughout this period, we facilitated a series of five discovery sessions with software developers, conducted 14 user interviews, and held eight user shadowing sessions. These activities allowed us to directly engage with the individuals who interacted with the Admin platform and gain valuable insights into their experiences. Additionally, we analyzed existing user data using tools like FullStory to further deepen our understanding of the issues affecting the Admin platform.
By actively involving stakeholders, conducting user research, and analyzing data, we were able to identify and compile a comprehensive set of problems and pain points that informed the development of Mercury. This meticulous process ensured that our project was grounded in a thorough understanding of the challenges faced by users of the Admin platform.

After identifying the key problems affecting the usage of the existing Admin platform, my project manager (PM) and I took the initiative to develop product strategies aimed at addressing these issues. During this process, we established three core principles that would guide our design work:
Addressing Multiple Problems with Fewer Designs:Our goal is to ensure that each redesign we undertake tackles multiple "How Might We" (HMW) problems simultaneously. This approach allows us to optimize our efforts and maximize the impact of each design iteration.
Strategic Use of Data for Design Problem Solving:We believe in making design decisions based on solid empirical evidence. To achieve this, we are committed to investing more time in discovery work. By conducting thorough research and analysis, we can ensure that every design decision we make effectively solves real business problems.
Future-Proofing Our Designs:Rather than focusing solely on short-term concerns, we aim to create designs that have long-lasting relevance. We want our designs to withstand the test of time and remain adaptable as new designers join the team to address future design challenges. This approach minimizes the need for massive redesigns in the future.
By adhering to these core principles, we strive to create impactful and efficient design solutions that address multiple problems, are backed by data, and can endure evolving needs and circumstances.
DefiningUnderstanding our usersTo build our product effectively, we conducted user research to understand their demographics, usage patterns, and aspirations. Utilizing both quantitative data, obtained from the People's team, and qualitative insights from interviews and user shadows, we gained a comprehensive understanding of our users. Through our analysis, we identified two main user categories with distinct sub-categories. This segmentation guided our product design and allowed us to tailor our solution to meet the specific needs and preferences of each user group, ensuring a more targeted and effective approach.

BlueprintNow that we know what problems we are solving, what design principles we are working on, and who we are building the product for, it is time to build the blueprint.
Renovating Information ArchitectureUpon evaluating the existing Admin portal, I analyzed its information architecture and identified a significant concern. The current structure allows users with varying rights and permissions to access all levels of information, including confidential data. This unrestricted access poses challenges regarding accountability and confidentiality. To address this issue, I have proposed a new information architecture that aims to streamline the information based on user segments. I believe that by implementing this approach, we can ensure that users only have access to the relevant information based on their specific roles and permissions. This enhancement will greatly enhance accountability and maintain the necessary level of confidentiality.
After developing this IA, I presented my findings to the platform team to receive buy-ins from the major stakeholders. Specifically, I needed to ensure that this IA meets the requirement of both my PM (Mohit), VP of Platform (Michael), and Head of Software (Thomas).

Defining User Rights and PermissionsAfter receiving buy-ins from my stakeholders, I organised a brainstorming session with 3 other software developers (Lily, Hasan, and Andy) to develop a user-permissions guide. In the session, we came out with a few different solutions to differentiate user rights. Our initial instinct was to differentiate it based on use cases. However, we realised that this was not a feasible solution because roles evolve.
"As a startup, roles always evolve and responsibilities change. It is important that we create more flexibility for our users by not limiting their rights on our platform." - Lily

After three months of conceptual work, we finally have created a feasible blueprint to build Mercury. I am proud to say that members of the Senior Leadership Team, the PM, our Lead Product Designer, and the Software Development team have signed off on this product blueprint. As a blueprint, it is not a definitive road ahead, but a valuable guide for us to rely on when we build the product.
Learning lesson:Keep an open mind; while having a structure like a blueprint helps, it is helpful to have some breathing space as new projects (like wash tracking) might be forced to take precedence due to changing business needs.
DesignWhen I initially joined Oxwash, we faced the challenge of not having a design system in place. To address this, my line manager and Lead Product Designer, Christie, and I took the initiative to develop the design system from scratch. Recognizing the importance of consistency and aligning with Oxwash's brand identity, we collaborated closely with the marketing and branding team.
Our first step was to create a research proposal to conduct in-depth brand research, with the aim of building a design system that truly embodied Oxwash's unique brand identity. To gather the necessary insights, we adopted a mixed-methods approach, combining qualitative and quantitative data collection methods. This approach allowed us to collect data on how both Oxwash employees and clients envisioned the visual representation of Oxwash.
The marketing team informed us that they were also planning to do a massive rebrand of Oxwash with the help of an external design agency. Thus, they wanted to use insights from our research to inform the agency's design decisions. By conducting intensive brand research, we aimed to create a design system that would reflect Oxwash's desired brand image and resonate with its target audience. The design system would serve as a set of guidelines to ensure consistency across all Oxwash products and materials, reinforcing the cohesive and recognizable brand identity we sought to establish.

Key findings from Brand Identity Research
The findings from our brand identity research played a vital role in shaping our design system work. They provided us with valuable insights that guided the philosophy behind our design decisions. To build our design system, we adopted an atomic design approach, breaking down components into their fundamental elements.
Whenever we encountered uncertainty regarding a design decision, we referred back to the findings from our brand identity research. This research served as a reliable reference point and helped us make informed choices. For example, when faced with the decision between using straight-lined or curvy elements for our Hero section, our brand identity research indicated that users preferred rounded components. This insight informed our design direction, as we aimed to create a more bubbly and youthful design interface that aligned with users' preferences.
Through this process, I discovered that although brand identity research may initially appear abstract, it holds tangible value when applied in the appropriate context. It provided us with an anchor for discussions and enabled us to make data-informed decisions rather than relying solely on inspiration. By grounding our design choices in empirical evidence, we ensured that our design system aligned with our users' expectations and preferences, ultimately enhancing the user experience.

On a monthly basis, we facilitate a Design Guild session where two front-end developers collaborate with us to build specific components within a "Storybook." This approach streamlines the implementation process, making it easier for developers to incorporate our design system into their work. In addition to our design system efforts, I took on the task of developing interaction guidelines. These guidelines serve as a reference for developers, ensuring consistent and cohesive implementation of our design across the Mercury platform. Throughout this process, close collaboration between the product design and software development teams was crucial to ensure the seamless integration of design and functionality in Mercury.
Road aheadWhile the rebuilding work represents a small step in enhancing operational efficiency at Oxwash, it is part of our broader mission to drive continuous improvement. Our focus extends beyond the design system alone, as we strive to innovate and create tools that directly impact operational efficiency. One such tool is Wash Tracking, which is designed to support our laundry talents in their work.
By providing them with a concrete design blueprint and actionable insights, we empower our team to move forward effectively. This commitment to innovation and efficiency will enable Oxwash to achieve its goals and deliver an exceptional experience for our customers and staff alike.
